This comprehensive review synthesizes findings from approximately 150 peer-reviewed studies to evaluate how crop diversity management, particularly through crop rotation and cover cropping, affects agricultural productivity, environmental sustainability, and economic outcomes. 
The research highlights that diversified cropping systems improve soil health, nutrient cycling, and water retention, leading to more stable and often increased yields over time. Crop rotation, especially when incorporating legumes, enhances soil fertility and reduces reliance on synthetic fertilizers, while also mitigating pest and disease pressures through ecological diversification. 
Environmentally, these practices contribute to reduced soil erosion, lower greenhouse gas emissions, and improved biodiversity across agricultural landscapes. Economically, the outcomes are more complex: while long-term benefits such as resilience and reduced input costs are evident, short-term profitability may vary depending on local conditions, crop choices, and management strategies. 
The review emphasizes that the effectiveness of crop diversity practices is highly context-dependent, influenced by factors such as climate, soil type, and regional farming systems.
It also identifies gaps in long-term data and calls for improved modeling approaches to better predict outcomes across different scales. 
Overall, the study positions crop diversity as a key strategy for achieving sustainable agriculture, balancing productivity with environmental and economic considerations.

Research on coffee consumption and its effects on health and well-being has expanded significantly, with most large-scale observational studies suggesting that moderate intake is generally associated with positive health outcomes. 
Reviews of the literature indicate that consuming approximately three to five cups per day is linked to reduced risks of several chronic conditions, including cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, stroke, and certain cancers, as well as lower overall mortality rates. 
Coffee contains numerous bioactive compounds, including caffeine and antioxidants such as polyphenols, which contribute to its anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties. 
In addition to physical health benefits, coffee consumption has been associated with improved cognitive performance, alertness, and mood, potentially lowering the risk of depression in some populations. 
However, these benefits are not universal, and excessive consumption can lead to adverse effects such as anxiety, sleep disturbances, increased heart rate, and gastrointestinal issues. 
The evidence base is largely observational, meaning causality is not always firmly established, and individual responses to coffee vary based on genetics, tolerance, and lifestyle factors. 
Overall, current research supports the view that coffee can be part of a healthy diet when consumed in moderation, while emphasizing the importance of personalized intake levels and further experimental studies to clarify long-term effects.
